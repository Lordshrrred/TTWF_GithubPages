Your car just died at 2 AM and you need a tow truck now. After-hours towing costs more than daytime service, but you can still find reasonable rates if you know where to look.

## What To Do

1. **Check your insurance first.** Many policies include 24-hour roadside assistance that covers towing up to a certain distance. This beats any cash rate you'll find.

2. **Call your credit card company.** Premium cards often provide emergency roadside service, including after-hours towing.

3. **Search "24 hour tow truck" plus your city name.** Skip the first few Google ads and look for local independent operators with physical addresses.

4. **Get quotes from 3-4 services.** Ask for the total estimated cost including base fee, mileage, and any after-hours surcharge. Prices vary wildly between companies.

5. **Ask about payment options.** Some accept credit cards, others cash only. Confirm before they arrive.

6. **Verify they can reach your location.** If you're on a busy highway or in a restricted area, not all tow trucks can legally access your car.

7. **Get the driver's name and truck number** when they arrive. Take a photo of their license plate for your records.

## What It Might Cost

Independent 24-hour services typically charge $85-120 for the hookup plus $3-7 per mile. After-hours surcharges add $15-35 to your base cost.

Highway towing through police dispatch costs significantly more. [Towing from highway to the nearest exit](/towing-cost-from-highway-to-nearest-exit/) often runs $150-250 total due to safety requirements and limited operator availability.

Your insurance roadside assistance might cover $100-150 of towing costs, making it essentially free for short distances. [Check how many times per year you can use services like GEICO](/geico-covers-towing-how-many-times-per-year/) to avoid surprise limitations.

Weekend and holiday rates increase costs 20-40% over normal after-hours pricing. [Sunday night towing services](/cheapest-tow-truck-service-near-me-sunday-night/) often charge premium rates when fewer operators work.

## Stay Safe

• Stay in your vehicle if you're on a busy road until the tow truck arrives
• Turn on hazard lights and use flares or reflectors if you have them
• Move to the passenger side when the driver approaches your window
• Never leave your car unattended with valuables visible
• Ask to see the driver's commercial license and insurance card
• Get a written estimate before authorizing any work beyond basic towing

Independent operators usually offer the best after-hours rates, but verify they're licensed and insured before letting them hook up your car. A few extra minutes of verification prevents bigger headaches later.

> **Quick Answer:** Smart drivers stock their vehicles with jumper cables or a portable jump starter, warning triangles or flares, a basic first aid kit, a reliable flashlight, a phone charger, a properly inflated spare tire with jack, and bottled water. These seven essentials handle most roadside emergencies. Unfortunately, most vehicles carry none of these items.

## Essential Items for Your Vehicle

### Trunk Storage (Permanent Items)

1. **Complete tire changing setup**, Include your spare tire, jack, and lug wrench. Verify spare tire pressure every six months. Low air pressure renders your backup tire worthless. Many modern vehicles come with temporary "donut" spares, so understand your limitations before trouble strikes.

2. **Battery boosting equipment**, Choose between traditional jumper cables (minimum 16-foot length) or a self-contained jump starter. Cables need another vehicle nearby, while jump starters work independently. For drivers who frequent isolated areas, the jump starter justifies its higher price.

3. **Warning devices for visibility**, Position reflective triangles or LED flares 100 to 300 feet behind your disabled vehicle. Standard practice calls for three triangles. These devices store compactly and never expire.

4. **Recovery equipment**, Pack a tow strap or rope rated for your vehicle's weight. This equipment helps extract your car from ditches, mud, or snow when other options fail.

5. **Repair tool collection**, Assemble flat and Phillips head screwdrivers, an adjustable wrench, needle-nose pliers, zip ties, and duct tape. These tools enable temporary fixes for common roadside problems.

### Passenger Area Storage

6. **Vehicle charging cable**, Keep a 12V or USB-C phone charger accessible. Your smartphone serves as navigation, emergency communication, and roadside service contact. Battery failure during a breakdown creates serious complications.

7. **Medical supplies**, Stock bandages, gauze pads, antiseptic wipes, medical tape, pain medication, and disposable gloves. Review expiration dates annually and replace outdated items.

8. **Illumination equipment**, Choose between battery-powered or USB-rechargeable flashlights. Nighttime tire changes, engine inspections, or emergency signaling require reliable lighting.

9. **Hard copy documentation**, Print insurance cards, registration papers, and roadside assistance contact information. Digital apps malfunction, but physical documents remain reliable.

### Extended Travel Additions

10. **Water supply**, Carry one gallon for radiator cooling (after engine cools), wound cleaning, or personal hydration during extended waits.

11. **Food provisions**, Pack energy bars or non-perishable snacks for situations requiring 2 to 4-hour waits for assistance.

12. **Emergency insulation**, Mylar survival blankets occupy minimal space while providing crucial warmth retention in cold conditions.

13. **Weather protection**, A basic rain poncho transforms tire changing from miserable to manageable during storms.

## Budget Considerations

Assembling a complete emergency kit costs between $100 and $200 when starting from scratch. Portable jump starters represent the largest single expense at $60 to $120. Remaining components like warning triangles, medical supplies, flashlights, tape, ties, and tow straps total under $80.

Most vehicle owners already possess some required items. Gather existing supplies into a dedicated storage bag, then purchase missing components. One emergency use will justify the entire investment.

## Safety Reminders

Store warning triangles and flashlights where you can reach them quickly, not buried beneath other cargo. Highway breakdowns at night demand immediate visibility measures within the first minute, not after unpacking your trunk contents.

Test spare tire pressure during spring and fall maintenance checks. Tires naturally lose 1 to 2 PSI monthly. After sitting unused for two years, most spare tires need air.

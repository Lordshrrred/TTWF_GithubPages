Getting into a car accident is stressful enough without worrying about towing expenses. The good news is that your auto insurance may cover these costs, but the answer depends entirely on your specific coverage and the accident circumstances.

> **The Bottom Line:** Auto insurance will pay for accident towing if you have collision or comprehensive coverage on your policy. Basic liability insurance won't cover it. When another driver causes the crash, their liability coverage should handle your towing expenses.

## Essential Steps to Take

**Contact your insurer immediately after the accident, before arranging towing services.** Insurance companies operate 24-hour claim hotlines specifically for these situations. They'll either send a contracted tow service or give you authorization to hire one for later reimbursement.

**Review your policy declarations to identify these key coverages:**
- *Collision protection*: pays for towing when accident damage makes your vehicle inoperable
- *Comprehensive protection*: covers towing for non-crash incidents like weather damage or vandalism
- *Roadside service add-on*: provides towing benefits for mechanical failures and other breakdown scenarios

**When someone else caused the accident,** their liability insurance becomes responsible for your towing bill. You'll work with their insurance carrier, not your own company.

**Create a complete record of everything.** Photograph your damaged vehicle, the accident location, and the tow truck with its company identification. Keep all receipts showing destination and final charges.

**Decide on your preferred repair facility before the tow truck arrives.** You have the right to choose any licensed shop for repairs. While insurance-preferred locations may speed up processing, you're not obligated to use them.

**Be cautious with police-arranged towing.** When officers call tow services to clear accident scenes, you remain financially responsible. If your car can wait safely, request permission to contact your insurance company's preferred provider instead.

## Coverage Breakdown and Costs

| Policy Type | Towing Benefits |
|---|---|
| Liability coverage only | No towing benefits |
| Collision/Comprehensive plans | Covered after deductible applies |
| Roadside assistance add-on | Covered without deductible |
| At-fault party's liability | Full coverage when they're responsible |

**Understanding deductibles matters:** When your collision deductible exceeds the towing cost, you pay everything. For example, a $200 tow with a $1,000 deductible means you cover the entire expense.

Standard insurance policies typically limit towing reimbursement to $75-$125 per incident. Any amount beyond this limit comes out of your pocket. Always verify your specific policy maximums.

## Safety Considerations

**Move to safety before handling insurance calls.** Physical safety trumps everything else at accident scenes. Get everyone away from traffic flow before dealing with coverage questions.

Watch for vehicle hazards like fluid leaks or engine smoke. Don't attempt moving damaged cars yourself, and inform tow operators about any dangerous conditions.

Stay alert for opportunistic businesses that target accident victims. Never sign documents you don't completely understand, especially authorization forms that some unethical tow companies push on shaken drivers.

Remember that collision coverage or at-fault driver liability may also provide rental car benefits while your vehicle undergoes repairs. Ask about this additional coverage when reporting your claim.
